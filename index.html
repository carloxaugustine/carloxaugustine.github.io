Aerodynamics · Structures · CFD

Deployable Tactical V-Wing UAV

OpenVSPVSPAEROOpenFOAMBlender

The Problem

Most fixed-wing ISR platforms are way too big or structurally complicated to actually carry and deploy in the field. I wanted to see if a V-wing configuration could be split into sections you could pack out and bolt together on-site without paying a huge aerodynamic penalty for the seams.

The reason I went with a flying wing specifically is that you get good span efficiency and no tail drag, which gives you some margin to absorb whatever penalty the seams add. The catch is that flying wings are inherently pitch unstable, so dihedral and sweep are doing a lot of work on the stability side.

Geometry and Configuration

I built everything in OpenVSP, starting with a central fuselage pod that the wing panels attach to. The pod is both the payload housing and the structural spine. I ran a bunch of VSPAERO iterations sweeping dihedral and sweep angle before landing on what you see in the render.

Getting the mesh to behave at the wing-pod junction took longer than I expected. OpenVSP's surface mesher produces irregular triangles at blended junctions and my early VSPAERO runs were throwing off-axis moments that didn't match my hand calcs. I had to go back and clean up those intersections before I trusted the panel results.

CFD and Seam Drag

The CFD ran in OpenFOAM using simpleFoam, steady-state incompressible RANS, k-omega SST turbulence model. The flow domain you can see in the mesh screenshot has the inlet about 3 chord lengths upstream and the outlet 8 chord lengths downstream.

I modeled the seams as a surface gap and step discontinuity at the panel joints. My first mesh was bad. The y+ values near the seam were way too high, over 100 in some spots, so the wall treatment wasn't actually resolving the boundary layer where I needed it. I refined the mesh locally around the seam features and reran. The velocity contour shown is from the corrected run.

Main finding: seam drag depends a lot on gap width and surface flush. A tight flush joint is manageable. A proud or recessed edge is way worse than the geometry would suggest because of the separation at the step.

Dihedral and Stability

I swept dihedral angle through VSPAERO and looked at static margin and Dutch roll characteristics at each point. More dihedral improves roll stability but pushes the neutral point forward and eats into pitch stability margin. The configuration in the render is the best tradeoff from that sweep.

What I'd Do Differently

Everything here is simulation only and the CFD hasn't been validated against any physical data. VSPAERO is also inviscid so it misses the separated flow regions I actually care most about. The real next step is building a scaled section and running it in a wind tunnel to get a validation point for the seam drag numbers.

I'd also redo the mesh with structured hexahedrals instead of snappyHexMesh. The unstructured approach is faster to set up but the near-wall resolution suffers for the same cell count, which matters a lot for this kind of boundary layer study.

ALBATROSS-1 Fixed-Wing UAV

Conceptual DesignAerodynamicsStructuresOpenVSP

The Project

ALBATROSS-1 was a full conceptual design study for a long-endurance low-altitude fixed-wing UAV. I wanted to actually work through a complete design cycle from mission requirements to a sized airframe rather than just sketch something and call it a concept.

Design Process

I started from a weight budget and mission profile and worked backward to size the wing using a simplified Breguet range equation. Wing geometry was iterated in OpenVSP to hit the required cruise lift coefficient while keeping the aspect ratio within structural limits for a composite spar.

Flight Operations · Systems · Python

Curiosity Rover Thermal Diagnostics

NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory — Mars Science Laboratory · Summer 2025
PythonSystems IntegrationOperational Data PipelinesElasticsearchAutomated Reporting

The Problem

On an active Mars mission, the thermal team has to keep rover components inside tight temperature limits, which means constant "preheat" diagnostics. That data was scattered across systems and validated by hand, and there was no standard way to see long-term trends — slow, and easy to get wrong.

What I Built

Three modular Python tools that together form one pipeline: pull the relevant heating events from the operations database, compute performance margins automatically, and generate the formatted tactical reports the thermal team posts. I replaced an older manual workflow with a native Python implementation and put the whole thing on an automated schedule so it runs without anyone touching it.

Working in Flight Ops

The harder half was integration. Everything had to slot into existing flight-operations infrastructure and match the team's conventions and config structures. I spent real time fighting environment and dependency issues across different machines before it ran cleanly end to end, working alongside thermal, operations, and systems engineers to get it right.

Impact

Every heating event now gets automatic margin and efficiency metrics and shows up in the daily operational reports with no manual data entry. The work is continuing toward a single consolidated thermal data index and a dashboard.

This project involves export-controlled mission data, so the description here is intentionally high-level. Technical specifics, schematics, and internal tooling are omitted.

Operational Forecast Cloud Port

NOAA — Center for Operational Oceanographic Products & Services · William M. Lapenta Fellowship · 2024
PythonRHPCnetCDF / GRIB2Interpolation

The Problem

NOAA's San Francisco Bay Operational Forecast System (SFBOFS) ran on an on-premise supercomputer. The goal was to get it running on the National Ocean Service cloud sandbox, which meant moving the model and reconciling some awkward data-format differences along the way.

HPC → Cloud Port

I ported the system from the on-prem NOAA HPC to the cloud sandbox, reusing the existing finite-element mesh and grid. From there I ran SFBOFS under different forcing conditions and looked at how water level, currents, temperature, and salinity responded — including a comparison of global-model versus regional-model forcing to see which performed better.

Data-Format Tooling: netCDF → GRIB2

A big chunk of the work was format conversion. I helped build one of the first netCDF-to-GRIB2 converters for the group, in Python and R. The hard part is regridding: the source data lives on an unstructured, CDF-style grid and GRIB2 wants a regular grid, so I implemented the interpolation — nearest-neighbor and bilinear — to move values across without distorting them. The plot above shows the original grid resampled onto the target grid.

Side Build: Sea Level Stripes

I also built a small tool that pulls hourly water-level data from the NOAA API, resamples it to monthly means, and renders long-term trends as "climate stripes" per tide station. The four-panel image shows relative sea level at La Jolla, San Diego, Honolulu, and Kodiak Island from the early 1900s to today.

California Current Ecosystem Modeling

Scripps Institution of Oceanography — CCE-LTER REU, Dr. Art Miller's lab · 2023
MATLABNumerical ModelsData VisualizationStatistics

The Work

I worked with output from a regional ocean model of the California Current — POP for the physics, COBALT biogeochemistry, MARBL ecology. I picked up MATLAB to read the large model-output files, then built seasonal-cycle "snapshots" and animations across temperature and the different plankton types: diatoms, small phytoplankton, coccolithophores, diazotrophs, and zooplankton.

What It Showed

By averaging by month and computing anomalies, I could line up physical drivers with ecological response: coldest coastal water during spring upwelling, diatom blooms tracking those upwelling nutrients, and clearly different offshore-versus-coastal phytoplankton regimes. The maps shown are from my own model output — temperature, diatoms, and zooplankton.
